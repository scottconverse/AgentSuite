## Key Topics

### Topic 1: Phishing and Social Engineering

**Why it matters for this audience:**
[Connect phishing risk to this specific audience's role and access level. Reference {{ threat_category }} where applicable.]

**What to cover:**
- How phishing emails look — real-world examples, not generic "look for bad grammar"
- Spear phishing: why personalized attacks are harder to spot
- Pretexting and vishing: attacks that come by phone or in person
- How to verify identity before sharing credentials or data
- What to do when you receive a suspicious message — the exact steps, not "be careful"

**Red flags to recognize:**
- Urgency or pressure ("Act now or your account will be suspended")
- Requests to bypass normal process ("Just this once, don't create a ticket")
- Mismatched sender address vs. display name
- Unexpected attachments or links, even from known contacts
- Requests for credentials, payment, or sensitive data via email

**The one rule:**
When in doubt, don't click. Call the person directly using a number you already have — not one from the suspicious message.

### Topic 3: Incident Reporting

**The cultural message:**
Report first, ask questions later. There is no penalty for reporting something that turns out to be harmless. There is a significant risk — to the organization and to individuals — in not reporting something that turns out to be an incident.

**What to cover:**
- What counts as a reportable security event
- How to report — exact steps, exact contact: {{ team_lead }}
- What happens after you report — so people know it doesn't disappear into a void
- Protection for good-faith reporters — no retaliation policy
- Timeline expectations: when will someone respond?
